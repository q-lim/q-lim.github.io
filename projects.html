Undergraduate Research Opportunities Programme, National University of Singapore
Summer 2021

Bone fractures are the 4th most prevalent cause of morbidity, and the risk of sustaining bone fractures is increased by diseased bone. Osteoblasts are cells which are responsible for the formation of bone tissue and can be produced by the differentiation of mesenchymal stem cells. Mesenchymal stem cells are essential to the fracture healing process as they proliferate in the haematoma before the formation of the soft callus and subsequently bridging bone before the bone is remodeled to its original shape. Hydroxyapatite is a bioceramic with the closest chemical similarity to natural bone and is currently used in scaffolds for bone tissue engineering. Silicon is an element involved in the early stages of bone calcification and has a structural and metabolic role in hard connective tissue in vivo. This study involves the substitution of silicon in the form of orthosilicic acid into hydroxyapatite to determine whether silicon-substituted hydroxyapatite is a more favourable material than hydroxyapatite for bone regeneration applications. Hydroxyapatite and silicon-substituted hydroxyapatite microspheres were produced, seeded with mesenchymal stem cells in vitro and the density of mesenchymal stem cells was compared over a 9-day period. Silicon-substituted hydroxyapatite microspheres showed a greater density of mesenchymal stem cells than the hydroxyapatite microspheres over this 9-day period. This shows more osteoblasts can be produced in vivo in the presence of silicon-substituted hydroxyapatite microsphere scaffolds, potentially shortening the fracture healing time and making silicon-substituted hydroxyapatite a more favourable material for bone regeneration applications.
